The Executive Pay Gap

The executive pay gap refers to the ratio between the compensation of top corporate executives — most often CEOs — and the average salary of their employees. This metric has become a central indicator in debates around economic inequality and corporate governance.

In 1965, U.S. CEOs at the largest firms earned around 20 times more than their typical worker. By the year 2000, this had exploded to over 350×. While some moderation occurred after the 2008 financial crisis, the ratio surged again during the 2010s and peaked above 390× in 2021.

What Drives the Gap?

Several structural factors have contributed to the widening of this pay ratio:

  • Stock-Based Compensation: CEOs often receive large parts of their pay in stock or stock options, which scale rapidly with market value.
  • Executive Bargaining Power: Boards often benchmark compensation competitively, pushing pay upward across industries.
  • Weak Wage Growth: Worker pay has stagnated, especially in low- and middle-income jobs, while executive pay has accelerated.

Global Comparisons

While the U.S. leads in CEO pay multiples, similar disparities exist worldwide. In the UK, the CEO-to-worker ratio sits around 100×; in Germany, it hovers closer to 70×. Many OECD countries have introduced disclosure requirements and corporate governance rules to address transparency.

Why It Matters

A growing executive pay gap can negatively affect morale, productivity, and long-term economic stability. It also contributes to broader wealth inequality. Critics argue that misaligned incentives between executives and the workforce reduce company resilience and public trust.
