Global Birth Rates Are Falling

A historic shift: fertility rates have halved, reshaping labor, economies, and our future.

Historic Decline

In 1960, families globally averaged about 5 children. Today, that figure is near 2.3 — a level close to or below replacement in many nations. This radical drop marks one of the largest demographic transitions in human history.

Regional Trends

While European and East Asian countries see record lows, Africa maintains high fertility — fueling a young, growing population. This demographic divergence will shape migration, trade, and global economic shifts.

Consequences & Risk

Low birth rates increase the old-age dependency ratio: fewer workers must support more retirees. Economists warn that shrinking workforces slow GDP growth and pressure pension and healthcare systems.

Policymakers debate how to respond: pro-natalist subsidies, immigration reform, and workplace flexibility. Yet, no country has fully reversed sustained decline once entrenched.
