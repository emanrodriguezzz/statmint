Global Birth Rates Are Falling — And the World Isn’t Ready

Global birth rates are steadily declining — a trend with major consequences for economies, labor markets, and social systems. From 1960 to 2020, the average number of births per 1,000 people dropped from 36.4 to just 18.5 (World Bank).

Fertility rates are even lower in many developed countries. South Korea hit a record low of 0.72 in 2023, and Japan has hovered near 1.3 for years. In the U.S., the birth rate was 11.0 per 1,000 in 2022 (CDC).

According to the UN Population Division, by 2050, 1 in 6 people globally will be over age 65. Fewer children now means fewer working-age people later — a shift that strains tax revenue, pension systems, and healthcare resources.

Why is this happening? Factors include access to education and contraception, changing family values, urbanization, and cost-of-living pressures. For example, raising a child in the U.S. now costs over $300,000 on average (WSJ).

Policymakers are trying to respond. Some countries have introduced incentives — like tax breaks and parental leave — but with limited success. The real solution may involve rethinking immigration, family planning, work-life balance, and education altogether.
