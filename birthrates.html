Global Birth Rates Are Falling

Fertility has declined nearly 50% since 1960 — reshaping economies and societies worldwide.

Over the past 60 years, the global birth rate has dropped from 36.4 births per 1,000 people in 1960 to just 18.5 in 2020. This demographic shift is impacting labor markets, social systems, and national economic plans.

According to the World Bank, key factors include industrialization, urbanization, and improved education for women. South Korea now reports the world’s lowest fertility rate at just 0.72 (2023).

Japan, Italy, and Germany face similar long-term declines, while the United States dropped from 14.4 births per 1,000 in 2000 to 11.0 in 2022. Emerging markets are also beginning to mirror this pattern.

A shrinking youth population can strain the workforce, dampen economic growth, and shift public policy on education, immigration, and retirement.

Long-Term Forecasts

United Nations projections suggest countries like Japan and South Korea could see population declines of more than 50% by 2100 — bringing significant economic and social challenges.

Sub-Saharan Africa remains an exception, driving much of the world’s population growth. Nigeria is expected to rank as the third largest country by 2050.

Countries are experimenting with solutions: France offers childcare benefits, Sweden boosts parental leave, and Singapore runs matchmaking initiatives. However, reversing the decline at scale remains elusive.
