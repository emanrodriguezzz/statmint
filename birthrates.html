Global Birth Rates Are Falling

Fertility rates have dropped for decades, reshaping demographics and economies worldwide.

Decades of Decline

In 1960, the global fertility rate was around 5 children per woman. Today, it is about 2.3, with dozens of countries far below the replacement level of 2.1. Japan, South Korea, and much of Europe now face aging populations and workforce shortages.

Regional Differences

While developed economies see fertility decline, Africa maintains high rates. Nigeria's fertility rate is over 5 and its population is projected to double by 2050. Asia shows contrasts: China and Japan are shrinking while India stays near replacement.

Economic Impact

Low birth rates reduce workforce size and tax revenue while increasing costs for pensions and healthcare. In 1950, there were seven workers per retiree in developed countries; by 2050, it will drop to about two. Policies like paid leave and subsidies help but rarely reverse the trend completely.

Looking Ahead

Demographic change influences everything from housing to innovation. Understanding birth rates helps forecast labor markets and economic growth. StatMint tracks these shifts to inform policy and public discussion.
