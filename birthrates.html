Global Birth Rates Are Falling

Fertility rates have dropped sharply, reshaping populations and economies worldwide.

Decades of Decline

In 1960, the global fertility rate was about 5 children per woman. By 2023, it is near 2.3. Many countries are well below the replacement rate of 2.1, which is needed to maintain stable population levels without immigration.

Japan’s population shrinks each year. South Korea reports the lowest fertility rate in the world at 0.72. Italy and Spain are also seeing steady declines despite government incentives to encourage larger families.

Regional Patterns

Low fertility is a challenge for high-income economies but less so for parts of Africa and South Asia. Nigeria’s fertility rate remains above 5, fueling rapid growth. India remains near replacement, and has surpassed China as the most populous country.

Population trends affect migration, economic power, and global markets. High-fertility regions will contribute most of the world’s workforce growth in coming decades.

Economic Impact

Countries with fewer young workers face slower economic growth and greater strain on social support systems. In 1950, there were seven workers for every retiree in developed nations. By 2050, it will be closer to two workers per retiree.

Governments use tax credits, paid leave, and childcare subsidies to try to raise birth rates. So far, no country has fully reversed a long-term decline.

Outlook

Low birth rates affect productivity, tax revenue, and innovation. Understanding these trends is key for planning investments, healthcare, and immigration policies. StatMint provides data and visual insights so that the public can better grasp these global shifts.
