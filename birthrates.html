Global Birth Rates Are Falling

Explore how falling fertility shapes economies, aging populations, and policy debates worldwide.

Decades of Decline

In 1960, the global fertility rate was about 5 children per woman. By 2023, it is around 2.3. Countries like Japan and South Korea now face shrinking populations and workforce gaps. Many European nations also sit well below the replacement level of 2.1.

Regional Patterns

High-income regions continue to decline, but Sub-Saharan Africa remains an exception. Nigeria's fertility rate stays above 5. India, meanwhile, maintains near replacement levels while China has dipped below. These differences affect global labor markets and migration trends.

Economic and Social Impact

Fewer young people means slower economic growth and more pressure on social safety nets. In 1950, seven workers supported one retiree; by 2050, it will be closer to two. Governments invest in paid leave, childcare support, and family incentives, but few have reversed the decline.
